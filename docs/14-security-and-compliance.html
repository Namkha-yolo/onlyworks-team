OnlyWorks Security & Compliance Plan

OnlyWorks captures screenshots of people's work. That is among the most sensitive data categories a SaaS product can handle. This doc is the plan for protecting it — technically, organizationally, and legally — and for being able to prove we protect it when enterprise buyers and regulators ask.

This is not a "we'll figure it out later" doc. The combination of screen capture + LLM processing + enterprise customers means three things will arrive together in months 4–9: security questionnaires from buyers, GDPR/CCPA scrutiny on the consumer side, and SOC 2 expectations from anyone larger than a 100-person company.

We need a plan that holds up to all three. This is it.

1 · Why this matters specifically for OnlyWorks

A normal B2B SaaS sees text, payloads, maybe documents. OnlyWorks sees everything on the user's screen: their Discord DMs, their bank login, their unfinished email to HR, an attorney's privileged comms, medical portals, customer PII visible in CRM tabs. Even with on-device OCR, the text of all of that is what we process.

Implications:

  • Data classification is elevated. Almost everything we touch is at least "confidential," and a lot of it incidentally crosses into "regulated" (PHI, PCI, attorney-client). Our default has to assume regulated until proven otherwise.
  • Sub-processor risk is amplified. Every external service that touches OCR text becomes a partial copy of the user's confidential workspace. Sending text to Gemini is sending Discord DMs to Google.
  • Consent is the load-bearing legal mechanism. Without granular, revocable, informed consent, we have no defensible legal basis under GDPR for almost anything we do.
  • A single screenshot leak is a brand-existential event. Not a "PR moment" — a "the product is over" moment. The technical controls need to assume this.

Everything in this doc flows from those four facts.

2 · Threat model

The five threats we plan against, ranked by likelihood × impact:

# Threat Why it's likely Worst-case impact
T1 A user's screenshot containing PII / regulated data is exposed via our backend or in a generated report Defaults are "capture everything"; users won't notice they captured an SSN Reportable breach, GDPR fine up to 4% revenue, brand-ending
T2 A user account is compromised; attacker accesses portfolio + historical capture metadata Credential reuse is universal; we'll be a target after we get enterprise traction Account-level data exposure, customer churn
T3 A sub-processor (Gemini, Supabase, vendor X) leaks OCR text from a misconfigured API Cross-vendor misconfig is a frequent breach root cause Reputational damage, contract violations
T4 An insider (us, a contractor, a future employee) exfiltrates user data Small team = high access; future contractors are the highest-risk vector Trust collapse, possibly criminal
T5 A prompt injection in OCR text causes Gemini to leak data from another user or perform an unintended action LLM injection is an active research frontier; defaults are unsafe Cross-user data leakage

These five threats drive the architecture, the controls, and the policies in the rest of this doc. If a proposed control doesn't reduce one of these, it's probably theater.

3 · The three layers

Security at OnlyWorks operates on three layers. They are different problems, require different tools, and often get conflated.

Layer What it covers Tooling Who owns it
AI safety What goes into and out of the LLM. PII filtering, jailbreak shields, hallucination controls, output redaction. On-device redaction + Presidio (or NeMo Guardrails) + Gemini safety settings Backend owner
Infosec / SOC 2 How the company protects data systemically: access control, encryption, logging, vendor mgmt, incident response, change mgmt. Vanta/Drata + 1Password + Sentry + GitHub policies Namkha (security lead)
Privacy / legal Lawful basis to capture, consent UX, right-to-delete, retention windows, DPAs, DPIA. Privacy policy + ToS + consent flow + retention engine + lawyer review Namkha + Backend owner

All three are required. None of them is "the security plan" on its own. Below, one section per layer.

4 · Layer 1 — AI safety: the capture-to-LLM pipeline

The defining technical pattern of OnlyWorks is on-device redaction before transmission. Sensitive material is filtered out before it leaves the user's machine. This is the only architecture that gives us a defensible story to GDPR, to buyers, and to ourselves.

The pipeline

[Screen capture (Electron + native APIs)]
   ↓
[Sensitive-app filter — by bundle ID / window title / URL]
   • Block: banks, password managers, healthcare portals, 1Password, Bitwarden,
            Chase, Wells Fargo, Kaiser, Epic, attorney-tools (Clio, MyCase),
            HR/payroll (Gusto, Rippling), tax software, Signal, WhatsApp
   • User can add custom rules
   ↓
[OCR — on-device only (macOS Vision / Windows.Media.Ocr)]
   • No image leaves the device for OCR
   • OCR text replaces the image; image is discarded after extraction
   ↓
[PII redaction pass — on OCR text]
   • SSN, credit card, US/EU/UK/CA phone, email, DOB, passport, IBAN, MRN
   • Implemented via Presidio REST or custom regex catalog
   ↓
[Sensitive-content classifier — on OCR text]
   • Medical (ICD codes, common drug names, condition keywords)
   • Legal (privileged-comm markers)
   • Financial (account numbers, routing, balance keywords)
   • If flagged: drop entire frame, log to user's privacy ledger
   ↓
[User review gate — optional, opt-in per session]
   • Daily-summary preview before send (user can strike entries)
   ↓
[Encrypted upload to backend]
   • TLS 1.3, application-layer envelope encryption for OCR text body
   ↓
[Input rail — on backend, before Gemini call]
   • Second-pass PII check (defense in depth)
   • Jailbreak shield (block known injection patterns)
   • Topic constraint (must look like work activity)
   ↓
[Gemini call]
   • Lowest-data prompt: send aggregate text, not per-frame
   • Use Google's data-not-used-for-training endpoint
   ↓
[Output rail — on response]
   • Strip any reflected PII (Gemini sometimes echoes)
   • Refuse-on-trigger if response matches abuse patterns
   ↓
[Persist generated report]
   • Encryption at rest (Supabase + bucket KMS)
   • RLS scoped to owning user_id
   ↓
[Render to user]

Every arrow in the pipeline is a control surface. Every drop is an opportunity for sensitive data to leak. The architecture is dense for a reason.

On-device redaction details

Sensitive-app filter — implemented in the Electron main process via active-win + a curated blocklist shipped with the app and synced from the backend. Users add rules in Privacy settings (onlyworks_desktop_v2/electron/capture/sensitive-filter.ts). The blocklist is permissive by default for known-sensitive categories and aggressive on user override.

PII detection — initial implementation uses Microsoft Presidio called via REST from a tiny Python/FastAPI sidecar bundled with the desktop app. Presidio gives us 20+ recognized entity types out of the box with country-aware variants. The alternative — hand-written regex — is acceptable for v2.0 but won't pass a sophisticated buyer's security review by v2.2.

Sensitive-content classifier — keyword + small fine-tuned classifier (DistilBERT or smaller, on-device). Flags medical / legal / financial markers. False positives drop frames; this is the right error direction.

User review gate — opt-in, surfaced on first session and in Settings. When enabled, the daily summary is held for user review for 24 hours before send. Users can strike specific entries. Crosses the gap between "trust us" and "watch us."

Input rail (before Gemini)

Defense in depth. Even though on-device redaction already ran, we re-check on the backend because:

  1. The user might have disabled on-device redaction.
  2. The redaction service may have failed silently (we'd rather drop than leak).
  3. Injection attacks can hide in OCR text in ways that look benign on-device.

Implementation: a thin guardrails layer in the backend (backend/services/rails.ts) that runs:

  • Presidio check (same engine as on-device)
  • Pattern match against a known-injection corpus
  • Topic classifier ("does this look like work activity?")

Failure mode: log + skip the call. We'd rather have an incomplete report than a leaked prompt.

Output rail (after Gemini)

Gemini sometimes echoes parts of the prompt back in the response. The output rail runs Presidio over the response and redacts any entities found before persisting. Also enforces structural constraints: the response must match the expected JSON schema for the report; anything else is dropped and logged.

Gemini configuration

  • Use data-no-train endpoint variant
  • safetySettings set to BLOCK_MEDIUM_AND_ABOVE across all harm categories
  • Sign a data processing agreement with Google for Gemini API
  • No fine-tuning on user data without explicit per-user opt-in

5 · NeMo Guardrails vs Node-native — decision matrix

For the input/output rails: should we use NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails, NVIDIA's hosted NIM, or a Node-native implementation?

Dimension NeMo Guardrails (Python service) NVIDIA NIM (hosted) Node-native (Presidio + custom)
Featureset Best — programmable rails, dialog control, retrieval safety, fact-check Same as NeMo Limited — PII + custom rules + Gemini safety only
Runtime Python — new service to deploy, monitor, scale NVIDIA-managed Node — runs inside our existing backend
Latency overhead 80–200ms (extra hop) 100–250ms (cross-network) 20–60ms (in-process or sidecar)
Ops burden High (new language, new service) Low (managed) Low (existing stack)
Cost Self-hosted (~$50–200/mo infra) Paid per call (~$0.001/req) Presidio REST (~free at our scale) + Gemini included
Team fit Poor — no Python on staff Fine Excellent — current stack
Vendor lock-in None (OSS) High (NVIDIA) None
SOC 2 story "We use NVIDIA NeMo Guardrails" — recognizable Same Has to be explained more
Auditability Strong (declarative rails in YAML) Strong Medium (code in TS)
Roadmap fit for v2.0 (months 1–3) ❌ Too much new infra to stand up ⚠ Possible if we commit to NVIDIA early ✅ Ships in two weeks
Roadmap fit for v2.2 (months 7–9, SOC 2 audit) ✅ If we have a security engineer ✅ If cost works at scale ⚠ Good but needs the "we evaluated NeMo" answer

Recommendation

v2.0: Node-native. Microsoft Presidio (REST) + custom rails in TypeScript + Gemini's built-in safety settings. Ships in two weeks. Defensible. Lets us iterate on rule logic with the team we have.

v2.2 (start eval at month 6): Re-evaluate NeMo Guardrails. By then we'll know whether the rails layer is a bottleneck (it will be more complex), whether we need declarative rails for compliance evidence, and whether we have a Python-comfortable engineer. If yes to two of three: switch.

Lock the decision via RFC before W3 of v2.0. Title: RFC-001: Guardrails strategy for the Gemini path.

6 · Layer 2 — Infosec and SOC 2

SOC 2 is not a tool. It's an audit against the AICPA's Trust Service Criteria, attesting that we say we do things and we actually do them.

The Trust Service Criteria

Criterion Required? What it means
Security (CC) Yes Protection against unauthorized access. The baseline.
Availability Optional but recommended Uptime, BC/DR — enterprise buyers want this
Processing Integrity Optional System processes accurately and completely
Confidentiality Optional but recommended for us Confidentiality of customer data (relevant given screenshots)
Privacy Optional Personal information handling

For OnlyWorks v2.2: scope to Security + Confidentiality. Add Availability when we have customers paying us enough to demand 99.9% SLAs.

The 15 policies we need

Vanta gives us templates. We adapt. We get a lawyer to read the final versions.

  1. Information Security Policy — overarching
  2. Access Control Policy — provisioning, deprovisioning, MFA, least privilege
  3. Acceptable Use Policy — what employees can and can't do with company assets
  4. Asset Management Policy — laptops, accounts, keys
  5. Change Management Policy — how code reaches production
  6. Data Classification & Handling Policy — public / internal / confidential / regulated
  7. Encryption Policy — at rest + in transit standards
  8. Incident Response Policy — sev levels, escalation, postmortem requirements
  9. Vendor Management Policy — how we vet subprocessors, DPA requirements
  10. Risk Assessment Policy — annual, documented
  11. Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery Policy — RTO/RPO targets
  12. Backup & Recovery Policy — backup cadence, restore testing
  13. Vulnerability Management Policy — scanning cadence, SLA for patching
  14. Secure Development Lifecycle Policy — code review, dependency scanning, secrets handling
  15. Privacy Policy — public-facing, separate from infosec but referenced

Controls

The policies say what we do. The controls are how we prove it.

Control area Implementation
MFA everywhere GitHub, Google Workspace, Supabase, Vercel/Cloudflare, AWS (if used), Stripe, 1Password — all require MFA. Enforced via SSO where possible.
SSO Google Workspace as the IdP. All vendors that support SSO use it.
Encryption at rest Supabase (Postgres + buckets) with KMS. Local user storage encrypted via Electron's safeStorage.
Encryption in transit TLS 1.3 minimum on every edge. HSTS preload.
Centralized logging Backend logs → Better Stack / Axiom / Datadog. Auth events, API calls, admin actions. 90-day retention minimum.
Code review enforcement GitHub branch protection: required reviewer, required CI pass, no direct push to main.
Secrets management Doppler or 1Password for engineering secrets. No secrets in env files committed to repo. Scanning via gitleaks in CI.
Vulnerability scanning Snyk or Dependabot on all repos. SLA: critical < 7 days, high < 30 days.
Pen test Annual third-party engagement starting v2.2. Cobalt or similar (~$15k).
Employee security training Annual. KnowBe4 or Vanta-bundled.
Background checks At hire. Checkr (~$30/person).
Endpoint security Laptops have full-disk encryption (FileVault / BitLocker), MDM for fleet ≥5 people.

7 · Layer 3 — Privacy and legal

Because we capture screenshots, our legal obligations are stricter than most B2B SaaS. The decisions here are not "should we" but "how exactly."

DPIA — Data Protection Impact Assessment

GDPR Article 35 requires a DPIA for "systematic monitoring" — and continuous screen capture is the textbook example. We need one before our first EU user signs up.

Template: ICO DPIA template. About a day of work. Outputs: documented assessment of risk + mitigations.

Lawful basis

For workers (the people running the desktop app): consent, granular and revocable. Must be:

  • Freely given (no consent-or-you-can't-use-the-product on optional features like training-data path)
  • Informed (clear in plain English what we capture, where it goes, who sees it)
  • Specific (separate consents for capture, AI processing, public profile, training data)
  • Revocable (one-click withdrawal in Settings)

For HMs / employers (if we move into B2B internal reporting in v2.2): legitimate interest + employee notice + employer DPA — but that path needs separate legal review and is out of scope for v2.0–v2.1.

Consent UX — the critical pattern

In onboarding (Step 4 of the 6-step flow), present four separate toggles, each defaulted off:

  1. Capture and process work activity (required to use OnlyWorks — this is the product)
  2. Generate AI-written reports of my activity (can be off; product still works)
  3. Make my verified portfolio publicly visible (off by default; on when user explicitly publishes)
  4. Allow my data to improve OnlyWorks training models (off by default; opt-in only)

Each toggle expands to plain-English explanation: what data, where it goes, who sees it, how to revoke. Granular log of consent stored server-side with timestamps. Revocation triggers deletion of corresponding data within 30 days.

Privacy policy + Terms of Service

Both must be public, both must be reviewed by a privacy lawyer (not a generalist) before launch. Budget: $3–5k for both, drafted then lawyer-reviewed.

Required sections in privacy policy:

  • What data we collect (categories, sources, purposes)
  • Lawful basis (granular)
  • Recipients (subprocessor list)
  • International transfers (SCCs if data leaves EU)
  • Retention periods (per data category)
  • User rights (access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection, automated decision-making opt-out)
  • DPO contact (if applicable — depends on scale)
  • Complaint route (data protection authority)
  • Update procedure

Data retention policy

Per data category, with deletion enforced by a scheduled job:

Data category Retention Notes
Raw screenshots Never persisted past OCR OCR replaces image; image discarded
OCR text + activity batches 90 days Default; user can request shorter
Session reports 24 months Unless user deletes
Daily reports 24 months Unless user deletes
Generated portfolio entries Indefinite (user-controlled) User owns these
Auth logs 12 months SOC 2 minimum
Billing records 7 years Legal requirement
Deleted account residuals 30 days then permanent purge Right-to-delete SLA

Implementation: a retention-engine cron service in the backend. Quarterly job to run + log proof of deletion. This proof of deletion is itself SOC 2 evidence.

Right to delete

One-click in Settings → "Delete my account and all data."

  • Soft-delete immediately (account inaccessible)
  • 30-day grace period (user can recover via signed email link)
  • Hard-delete at day 31: rows + storage + analytics events + backups (next backup cycle excludes deleted users)
  • Email confirmation at start + end

Subprocessor change process

Adding a new vendor:

  1. Vendor security review (security questionnaire if no SOC 2)
  2. DPA signed before any production data flows
  3. Updated on public subprocessor page
  4. Existing customers notified by email (30-day notice for material additions)
  5. Added to Vanta vendor inventory

Removing a subprocessor:

  1. Data migration plan documented
  2. Proof of deletion from old vendor
  3. Public subprocessor page updated

11 · Trust messaging to enterprise buyers

By month 4 we need to be able to send buyers a clean "Security at OnlyWorks" one-pager. Outline:

  • Data handling — on-device redaction, what leaves the device, what doesn't, encryption in transit + at rest
  • Subprocessor list — public, current, change-notification process
  • Access controls — MFA, SSO, least privilege, audit logging
  • Vendor security — DPAs with all subprocessors, vetting process
  • Privacy — granular consent, retention windows, right-to-delete SLA, GDPR/CCPA stance
  • Compliance — SOC 2 Type 1 by month 6 (Type 2 by month 12); security questionnaire-ready
  • Incident response — sev levels, notification commitments (24h for sev-1 affecting customer data)
  • Contact — security@onlyworks.com + PGP key

This goes on a public trust portal (onlyworks.com/trust) by month 4.

Security questionnaire library

We will be asked to answer:

  • SIG (Standard Information Gathering)
  • CAIQ (Consensus Assessments Initiative Questionnaire)
  • VSA (Vendor Security Assessment)
  • Custom buyer questionnaires (every enterprise has their own)

Vanta auto-fills 60–80% of standard ones. We finish the rest manually. Maintain answers in a versioned doc so we never re-do work.

14 · Maintenance

  • A new vendor or subprocessor is added
