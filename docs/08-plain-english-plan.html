The Plan, in Plain English

If you only read one document, read this one.

What we're building

OnlyWorks captures what you work on, turns it into a verified record of your skills, and makes money in three different ways.

You install OnlyWorks. It runs quietly in the background while you build a project, work at your job, or do an internship. It takes occasional screenshots, extracts text from them with your computer's own OCR (so screenshots don't have to leave your computer for the basic features to work), and sends a small summary to our backend. Our AI reads those summaries and builds a profile of you: what skills you used, what you accomplished, what you're good at — with evidence to back it up.

That profile does three jobs at once:

  1. It gets you hired. Hiring managers pay us to search a pool of people whose skills are verified by their actual work. When one of them is looking for someone like you, you get a private message. No applying. No spam. They reach out to you because your portfolio shows you actually did the work.

  2. It writes your status reports. (This is the team feature — coming later, in v2.2.) Companies pay us per-seat. Every Friday, OnlyWorks gives each employee a draft of "what I did this week" written from their actual work. The employee reviews it and sends it to their manager. Everyone saves 30 minutes of writing-time and gets a more accurate report than what they would have remembered to type. The manager never sees the raw screenshots — only the AI summary the employee approved.

  3. It builds you a portfolio. (The career artifact.) Public profile at onlyworks.com/ow/yourID showing your verified skills with evidence. Share it on resumes, LinkedIn, applications, anywhere.

The same capture engine powers all three. We collect data once. Output three valuable things.

Who it's for

Three different people, all using the same product:

Students and early-career people (the biggest opportunity). A college CS student building a hackathon project, a design student doing their senior thesis, a bootcamp grad with three portfolio projects, an intern at a startup. They have time to use the product. They desperately want to get hired. They have no employer telling them they can't install a screen-capture tool. Their resume is thin. This is the canonical user — think of Brodey.

Mid-career professionals (3–15 years). Currently employed engineers, designers, PMs, marketing managers. They're "open to opportunities" — 70% of LinkedIn falls in this category. They want a passive way to maintain a verified career history. They use OnlyWorks daily but barely think about it. When promo season hits, they have 26+ weeks of summaries already written. Maybe a recruiter pings them.

Companies (later, v2.2). Teams that hate the weekly status-report ritual. Eng managers, agency leads, distributed teams. They pay per-seat to give every employee an "auto-status-report writer."

Hiring managers are the secondary user — they pay us. They search candidates whose verified skills are based on actual work, not LinkedIn fluff. Best for companies hiring early-career talent where resume signal is noisiest.

How it makes money

We don't charge most workers anything. We charge the people who want access to workers.

Who pays What they get Price
Worker (default) Capture + portfolio + matched-by-HM Free
Worker (Pro upgrade) Unlimited reports, custom features, PDF export $9/mo
Student (verified .edu) Everything in Pro Free
Hiring manager (Starter) 5 job posts, 25 reach-outs/mo, full search $99/mo
Hiring manager (Pro) Unlimited posts + reach-outs, saved searches $299/mo
Hiring manager (Team) 3 recruiter seats, ATS integration $599/mo
Team (B2B, v2.2) Auto-status reports for the whole team 9−19/seat/mo

Critically: None of this works today. Stripe isn't integrated in the existing codebase. The paid hiring tier exists in our database as a placeholder column but there's no checkout flow, no webhook, no way to actually become a paying customer. Building this is the #1 thing that unlocks revenue. Without it, everything else is theater.

Why this product matters now

A few things are true in 2026:

  • Microsoft Recall was killed and Rewind was acquired-and-killed by Meta. Passive screen capture for self-quantification has a bad reputation right now. We can do this right — local-first OCR, user-owned data, sensitive-content filtering, no surveillance framing — and ride the wave when better positioning lands.
  • Hiring is broken for early-career candidates. LinkedIn endorsements are noise. GitHub commits are gameable. AI-written resumes are everywhere. Verified-by-capture is a fresh trust signal. The first company to nail it wins.
  • Status reports waste 30 minutes per employee per week. Every Slack standup, every Monday morning ritual, every Friday wrap-up. We can replace that with one click of "approve and send."
  • PointOne (legal AI billing) just raised $16M Series A on a 10x revenue growth. They proved the "passive desktop capture → AI output" model works at $30+ ARPU. We're applying the same pattern to hiring and team reporting.

What we're actually building in the next 12 weeks

Not all three revenue streams at once. Just enough to get the first one working.

v2.0 (Months 1-3) — Foundation + the marketplace

  • Brand-new desktop app: Electron + React + TypeScript + modern stack
  • One capture engine that does everything (instead of v1's two parallel pipelines)
  • Five screens: Status, Portfolio, Privacy, Settings, First-Run Onboarding
  • Backend: Stripe integration so HMs can actually pay
  • Web: Hiring manager dashboard, public profile, job post creation
  • Soft launch to ~14 existing users + 30 new students + 5 paid HM pilots

v2.1 (Months 4-6) — Worker utility

  • Promo packet generator
  • Weekly summary email (Fridays)
  • LinkedIn / Google OAuth at signup
  • Saved searches for HMs
  • Job-match notifications in the desktop

v2.2 (Months 7-9) — The B2B team feature

  • Rebuild the team UI with strict privacy boundaries: employees own + approve their own reports
  • Status report templates (weekly, sprint retro, 1:1 prep, OKR, quarterly)
  • Slack + email delivery
  • Per-seat Stripe billing for teams
  • This is the second revenue stream

What success looks like in 12 weeks

By end of week 12:

  • 100+ workers using OnlyWorks every week (at least 60% from outside our network)
  • 5 paid hiring managers (500−1,500 monthly recurring revenue)
  • 80% of new users opt-in to training-data sharing (clear signal that our consent UX works)
  • 30+ student users via 1-2 campus partnerships or a hackathon
  • 1M+ training frames collected with proper consent metadata
  • Zero capture-pipeline bug reports in the final 4 weeks

This is small. It's deliberately small. The point of v2.0 isn't to hit hockey-stick growth. The point is to ship a product that can be sold and can grow, with real Stripe revenue actually arriving in a bank account.

What could kill this

I'm being honest with you about the risks, not hiding them.

  1. The marketplace has a chicken-and-egg problem. No paying HMs means workers don't see hiring outcomes; no workers means HMs have nothing to search. The plan: Namkha personally lands the first 5 paid HMs from her network. Students are easier — campus partnerships have viral spread.

  2. Stripe webhooks are silent killers. If we miss a customer.subscription.created event, an HM pays but stays locked out. Mitigation: idempotent design, daily reconciliation script, fail-loud logging.

  3. Backend cost gets expensive fast. Every user running OnlyWorks 8 hours/day generates AI calls. Vision is capped at $0.50/user/day. Text reports aren't capped. Stale-session finalizer can double-charge on abandoned sessions. We need a hard per-user monthly AI budget cap before launch.

  4. Privacy gets us in trouble. Screen capture is creepy if framed wrong. The training-data consent flow has to be impossible to miss. The filtering of sensitive content (passwords, banking, private DMs) has to be visible and trustworthy. If we get this wrong on Hacker News, we get crushed.

  5. The B2B team feature is dangerous if we're sloppy. If we ever cross the line into "manager sees raw captures," we become bossware overnight and the D2C marketplace dies. The design principle is: employees own their data, employees approve their reports, managers see digests only. No exceptions.

  6. Intern velocity might be slower than expected. They have to learn a new stack. The plan: each intern builds a tiny "todo app" in the same stack before touching OnlyWorks code. Then Namkha reviews every PR for the first 4 weeks. Pair programming twice a week per intern.

What we're explicitly NOT doing

This list matters as much as the "doing" list, because every "no" gives us back time.

  • Not building manager-watches-employee features in v2.0. That's the bossware trap. The team feature comes back in v2.2 with a completely different positioning ("auto-status-reports for the employee to approve") and rigid privacy guarantees.
  • Not building billing/invoice automation. That's a different ICP (solo freelancers) and a different product. Park it. Maybe v3, maybe never.
  • Not building assignment-based skill verification ("upload resume → AI generates assignments → you do them → AI grades"). The captured-work approach is sufficient for now. If hiring managers tell us they don't trust the verified-by-capture signal, we'll add this in v2.2.
  • Not building resume upload + AI parsing. Tangential to the core story. Defer.
  • Not building Google or LinkedIn OAuth at signup. Magic-link email is enough for v2.0. Add OAuth in v2.1 only if magic-link conversion is bad.
  • Not building a Goals page. It was half-built in v1 and showed no PMF signal.
  • Not making the Activity page a separate top-level nav. Roll it into Portfolio.
  • Not adding 5/month custom report caps as a paywall. That was the wrong unit of value. Reports aren't what people pay for. Hiring outcomes are.

What you decide before any code lands

Seven things. Most are recommendations from the planning docs that just need a thumbs-up.

  1. Where the new code lives. Recommend: new onlyworks_desktop_v2/ directory in the existing repo. Merge to main when v2.0 ships.
  2. What we call it. Recommend: continue version line — call it "OnlyWorks 4.0." No rebrand.
  3. How current users get the update. Recommend: opt-in beta channel until 4 weeks bug-free. Then flip default.
  4. HM pricing. Recommend: $99 / $299 / $599. Lock it.
  5. Student tier. Recommend: free Pro for verified .edu email. Yes or no?
  6. Training data Terms & Conditions. Recommend: light legal review by week 4. Write the consent language in plain English.
  7. Intern ramp time. Recommend: 1 week building a tiny template app before touching OnlyWorks code. Each intern. No exceptions.

The thing I keep coming back to

You said earlier that the people who tested OnlyWorks outside your network came to it for "building resume, verifying skills, and hopes of getting hired by the managers on our platform."

That's the whole product story. Three things, in that order:

  1. Build a resume that's actually true. Verified by your work, not your claims.
  2. Verify your skills with evidence. Not "I list React on my LinkedIn." Show me 14 sessions where I used React.
  3. Get hired without applying. Hiring managers come to you because they trust what they see.

If we can make those three things real for a student or an early-career worker, we win. Everything else in this plan is in service of that.

What I need from you next

In rough priority order:

  1. Sign off on the 7 decisions above. Once they're locked, the team has clear permission to ship.
  2. Talk to 10 hiring managers in the next 4 weeks. While the team builds, you validate. The product won't matter if the HM side has no demand.
  3. Get on a call with the 5–10 strangers who tested v1. Ask what they wanted that we didn't deliver. They are the closest thing you have to a customer council.
  4. Decide whether you personally land the first 5 paid HMs (recommend: yes) or whether one of the interns is paired with you on sales (recommend: no, founder-sales matters at this stage).
  5. Be willing to kill features that aren't working. The biggest risk to v2.0 isn't building too little; it's building too much. Every "yes" to scope is a "no" to launch date.

That's the plan. Push back on anything that doesn't feel right.
